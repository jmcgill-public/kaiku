# The Helka Expanse — Tutorial Worldbuilding

This document provides the narrative backdrop for the Kaamos Kafka tutorial.
The world exists to make the event schemas interesting and the scenarios
meaningful. It is a teaching tool, not a complete fiction.

The deeper lore — the novel *Eerikki muistaa*, its characters, the folklore
foundation, the cosmological architecture — lives in a separate repository.

---

## The setting

The Helka Expanse is the oldest and most active sector of a galaxy-scale
shipping network. It is named for the *Helkavirsiä* — the ancient songs.
Ships move between refueling stations carrying cargo, passengers, and hehku
through routes that pass through a deep void region called Hiljaisuus —
the Silence.

Nobody fully understands why the routes are where they are. The Accord
maintains them the way you maintain infrastructure you depend on but don't
understand — carefully, superstitiously, with great anxiety about what would
happen if it stopped working.

---

## Hehku — the fuel

*Hehku* — Finnish for glow, radiance, the heat of something burning from
within.

The resource that powers everything in the Helka Expanse. Its origin is
unknown. It flows through the routes between stations. It is not scarce in
the way a mine runs out — it is effectively inexhaustible. The scarcity is
topological, not geological.

Everything passes through Hiljaisuus. The routes are where they are. Disrupt
the chokepoint and the flow stops. The hehku still exists. It simply cannot
reach where it needs to go.

That is the entire economy of the Expanse in one paragraph.

## The factions

**The Kalevala Accord** — the governing body of the Expanse. Maintains the
routes, regulates the stations, argues about hehku policy. Fading authority.
Maintains the forms of governance without always having the substance.

**Pohjan Neito** — the Northern Maiden. Controls rationing at critical
stations when disruption hits. Named for something older than the Accord.

**The Thousand Eyes** — present everywhere, acknowledged by no one. Nobody
knows what they want. They're always watching.

---

## The fuel volatility model

When Hiljaisuus is disrupted:

1. Hehku flow across the Expanse is choked
2. Stations begin drawing down reserves faster than resupply
3. Pohjan Neito begins rationing at critical stations
4. Prices spike at all stations — proportional to their dependency on
   Hiljaisuus routes
5. When the disruption clears, prices normalize — but not to baseline.
   The market has memory.

This is the scenario the tutorial producer demonstrates. The consumer and
Torakka state service observe and persist the cascade.

## A note on the wider fiction

The Helka Expanse originated as the backdrop for the novel *Eerikki muistaa*
— a space opera set in this universe. The tutorial uses the world's economic
infrastructure as a teaching backdrop. The novel uses the world's soul.

They are related but separate. The tutorial will not spoil the novel.
The novel will not require the tutorial.

## The ur-myth — corrupted history

### The thing nobody knows
There is no Zephram Cochrane. No famous scientist, astronaut, political
thinker whose name is attached to humanity's evolution from one world to
space travel, to the diaspora, to the discovery of brane travel. All of
that happened so long ago that the origins of humanity are completely
forgotten. What is remembered has already become the stuff that gets
laughed off as religion and myth and woo.

Even where people seriously believe in religion — which happens on worlds
— they never have any conscious thought about where they came from. How
they conquered space. The origin is gone. What remains is the story of
the origin, worn into myth, worn further into embarrassing folk belief
that educated people find quaint and serious believers hold without
examining too closely.

### Earth
The least auspicious planet in the universe. Between the author and the
reader: it's where all these people came from. This is never stated in
the novel. It is obvious to the reader who is paying attention and
irrelevant to everyone else.

### The Finns
The people who eventually conquered space. Who discovered the brane.
Who first encountered hehku. This is also never stated. It is in the
shape of the language, the names, the cosmology, the register. The
reader who knows will know. The reader who doesn't will feel it without
being able to name it.

### The plateau
Before brane travel, human civilizations in regular space were at a
plateau for a very long time. Interplanetary, maybe interstellar within
reach. Technological as they could be within regular physics. The plateau
lasted long enough that it became the assumed condition of existence.
Then brane travel was discovered and everything changed.

Hehku changed everything. It had the potential to end all strife, hunger,
shortage. Worlds never got past their ceiling with it. The amount of
hehku worlds use is minuscule. The stationborn marvel at this — we may
be running short in the void travel register but the worlds could have
an endless supply if only they could use it correctly. They stop short
every time. Every world. The ceiling Reko and Turo couldn't name.

### The ur-myth
Every world has a version of the same myth. Details vary. The shape is
identical. A people from a cold place at the edge of everything. A
discovery that shouldn't have been possible. A departure. A gift of
abundance that came with a catch nobody fully understood. Beings who
moved between worlds and didn't stay. A silence at the center of
everything that has a name.

*Hiljaisuus.*

The silence has been in the myths the whole time. Not as a place. As
the thing that was there before the departure. The thing they left
behind, or carried with them, or that followed without being invited.

### What the myth actually is
Not myth. Corrupted history. The story of how humans left one world,
discovered something that changed everything, built a civilization in
the void — that story is so old it has gone through the full cycle:

*History → legend → myth → religion → embarrassing folk belief*

The names have changed. The technology is described in the vocabulary
of magic because the vocabulary of the technology that produced it has
been lost so long that nobody remembers it was technology. The singers
who carried the story forward didn't know they were carrying history.
They thought they were singing about gods.

They were singing about the stationborn.
They were singing about the void.
They were singing about hehku, which they called by other names, all
of which meant the same thing: the gift that came from beyond, that
glowed, that could end all want, that nobody fully understood.

### The stationborn relationship to the ur-myth
They learn the comparative mythology in school. Find it mildly
interesting the way you find mildly interesting that multiple unconnected
cultures independently invented the wheel. Convergent cultural evolution.
Explicable. Moving on.

Reko and Turo had this class. Neither of them remembers much of it.

### What Selli knows
It isn't convergent cultural evolution.

The ur-myth is the same on every world because it is the same event
on every world. The departure. The discovery. The diaspora. The hehku.
All of it happened once and the echo of it is in every worldborn
culture because every worldborn culture descended from the people
it happened to.

Selli knows this.

The vocabulary required to say it out loud is the vocabulary of
worldborn superstition.

Selli won't say it.

The paddle clicks.

### Why this is everything
The myth that started this novel — the Kalevala, the runolaulu, the
Finnish oral tradition that Lönnrot collected in Karelia in the 1830s
— is in the ur-myth. Not as an influence. As the origin. The songs
Väinämöinen sang. The kantele made from a giant pike's jawbone. The
departure across water toward something unknown. The silence at the
edge of the world that has always been there.

Hiljaisuus.

The novel's backdrop is not inspired by Finnish mythology.
The novel's backdrop *is* Finnish mythology, at sufficient distance
in time that it has become the ur-myth that every world carries
without knowing where it came from.

This is what the author meant when he said the Kalevala was always there.
This is the mythological foundation becoming a real universe.
This is the exercise completing itself.

## Worldborn epistemology and the ur-myth

### The three epistemic scales

**Worldborn** — a life is seventy, eighty years. A generation is twenty.
A civilization is a thousand years of noisy information. Present tense to
the worldborn. Ancient history to everyone else before the last person who
lived it has finished dying.

**Stationborn** — worldborn generations are seasons. Civilizations are
mildly interesting weather patterns. The individual worldborn is invisible
at this scale. The swarm has patterns. The patterns are noted. The individual
resolves into nothing.

**Voidbound** — Eerikki has watched stationborn cultures drift. The Accord
that exists now is not the Accord he first encountered. He remembers when
current assumptions weren't obvious. This makes him slightly uncanny —
he is measuring the present against something stationborn can't see.

**Ara** — the brane itself has rhythms from her frame. The routes through
Hiljaisuus are not permanent. She watched them settle into their current
configuration. She knows what came before. She knows what will come after.
She knows the boundary Eerikki is looking for is not where he thinks it is.
She knows what it actually is. He hasn't asked the right question.

### The stationborn as gods

Worldborn myths describe beings who live forever, move between worlds,
are indifferent to human suffering not from cruelty but from scale
incompatibility. Who intervene seemingly at random. Who have their own
concerns that leave permanent marks on worldborn history without apparent
intention.

They made those myths up. Out of need. Out of terror of the indifferent
universe.

And then there are the stationborn.

Who are not gods. Who know they are not gods. Who have shift work and
irritating colleagues and the specific anxiety of people who live close
to hard vacuum. Who die, eventually, just more slowly.

But from the worldborn frame: these beings live forever, move between
worlds without aging, are indifferent to worldborn concerns, appear in
worldborn history and vanish, leave permanent marks without intention.

The myth fits. Perfectly.

The stationborn find this absolutely mortifying. Reko and Turo would
find it hilarious for thirty seconds and then it would start to bother
them and they'd change the subject. Selli has a joke about it. Has
never told it. The vocabulary required costs too much.

Eerikki doesn't find it mortifying. He finds it clarifying. He was
worldborn once. He has been on both sides of this mirror.

### The ur-myth — corrupted history

Every world has a set of myths that serious people laugh off as
superstition. Ancient beings from a cold place at the edge of everything.
A discovery that shouldn't have been possible. A departure. A gift of
abundance that came with a catch nobody fully understood. A silence at
the center of everything that has a name.

These are not myths. They are corrupted history.

The story of how humans left one world — probably Earth, probably the
least auspicious planet in the universe, the origin nobody remembers —
discovered the brane, found the hehku, built the diaspora — that story
is so old it has completed the full cycle. History to legend to myth to
religion to embarrassing folk belief that educated people find quaint.

The Finns who left. The discovery of the brane. The first hehku. All
of it is in the myths, scrambled, with the names changed and the
technology described in the vocabulary of magic because the vocabulary
of the technology has been lost for so long nobody remembers it was
technology.

Every world has a version. The details vary. The shape is identical.

The stationborn learn comparative mythology in school. Find it mildly
interesting. Convergent cultural evolution. Explicable. Moving on.

Selli knows it isn't convergent cultural evolution.

Won't say.

### The ceiling — why worlds never get past it

Brane travel changed everything. It brought hehku — the potential to
end all strife and hunger and shortage. Worlds never got past their
ceiling with it. The amount of hehku worlds use is minuscule. The
stationborn marvel at this.

The void may be running short. The worlds could have endless supply
if only they used it right. They never do. They stop. Every world.
Every time.

The ceiling is in the myths too. The catch nobody fully understood.
It's been there from the beginning, described in the vocabulary of
curse and taboo and divine prohibition, which are the words for
*we don't know why this happens but it always happens* in a language
that has forgotten it once had better words.

### Hiljaisuus — in the myths

The silence has been in the myths the whole time. Not as a place.
As the thing that was there before the departure. The thing they
left behind or carried with them or that followed without being
invited. The silence at the center of everything.

It has a name in every worldborn tradition. Different words.
The same silence.
