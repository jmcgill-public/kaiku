## The Helka Expanse

The oldest and most active sector of a galaxy-scale shipping network. Named for the
*Helkavirsiä* — the ancient songs.

Ships move between stations, carrying cargo, passengers, and hehku through routes that
pass through a deep void region called Hiljaisuus — the Silence. Nobody fully understands
why the routes are where they are. The Accord maintains them the way you maintain
infrastructure you depend on but don't understand — carefully, superstitiously, with
great anxiety about what would happen if it stopped working.

---

## Hehku

*Hehku* — Finnish: glow, radiance, the heat of something burning from within.

The resource that powers everything in the Helka Expanse. Its origin is unknown. It flows
through the routes between stations. It is not scarce in the way a mine runs out — it is
effectively inexhaustible. The scarcity is topological, not geological.

It is purple. Aava sees this directly. Others see it instrumentally.

Everything passes through Hiljaisuus. The routes are where they are. Disrupt the chokepoint
and the flow stops. The hehku still exists. It simply cannot reach where it needs to go.

Do not call it fuel in the prose. It is hehku.

The skin of Ananta Shesha. The membrane as the body of something vast.

---

## The void and the brane

The void has its own physics. It is a mystery. Do not explain it.

Travel in the void extends life. This is a social consequence, not a plot device.
Handle obliquely.

The brane is the membrane between void and normal space. Hehku flows through it where
the geometry allows. Nobody understands why the routes are where they are. The 21st
century physics that might have answered this never got far enough before people left
the worlds behind.

Ships do not dock at stations physically — they exist in different registers. Shuttle
vessels operate in the brane as interface. The threshold people who pilot them live in
the between.

Causality without time: the void has no clock but it has sequence. Emo doesn't record
timestamps. She records sequence.

---

## Hiljaisuus

The chokepoint. Everything passes through it. A deep void region between stations.

Named: the Silence.

Has been in worldborn myths the whole time — not as a place, but as the silence at the
center of everything. It has a name in every worldborn tradition. Different words.
The same silence.

The routes through Hiljaisuus are not permanent. Ara watched them settle into their
current configuration. She knows what came before. She knows what will come after.
She knows this, and she is not in any record at all.

## The three types of people

**Worldborn**

Short natural lives. Far from the void. The void's life extension doesn't reach them.
To stationborn they come and go like wildlife — generational swarms, not individuals.
Worlds have technology, use hehku for machines and medicine and food, but never get
past a ceiling toward greater abundance. Every world. Every time. The ceiling is in
their myths as curse and taboo — they don't know it was once better described.

**Stationborn**

Long lives from void-adjacency. Not the hehku itself — the proximity to the brane.
If a stationborn goes to a world long enough, they begin aging like a worlder.
The clock doesn't stop — it slows gradually, then restarts if they leave.

Fatalistic about life and death. Technologically fluent. Some adopt worldborn religious
fragments ironically. Some become genuine believers. The stationborn are the gods in
worldborn mythology — they find this mortifying.

**Voidborn**

Born in the void or shaped by it before anything else. No concept of time, scarcity,
or abundance. Birth and life and death as joik — the thing itself, not about the thing.
No frame of reference for being otherwise.

Pretty normal people actually, with a completely different operating system.

Slip away from stationborn before being seen — not fear; the weight of stationborn
attention disturbs them. Stay near Aava because her attention rests alongside rather
than landing.

## The stations

**Tuonela Station** — the oldest, most established. Named for the Finnish realm of the dead.
Has seen everything before.

**Manala Depot** — the underworld's warehouse. Functional, utilitarian, essential.

**Ikävä Anchorage** — named for longing. A waypoint people pass through on the way
to somewhere else.

**Usher Reach** — middling, declining slowly. Everyone who lives there knows it. Nobody
says it directly. Most interfaced with the void. Where Selli is. Where Eerikki returns.

**Diotima Platform** — reaches for something it can't quite touch.

## The Pohja corridor — Usher Reach

Named for *pohja* — Finnish: bottom, foundation, the deep north. Also resonates with
*pooja* — Sanskrit: offering, ritual approach to the sacred.

A dangerous place. The danger is never described. It runs closest to the brane interface.
Kaija keeps going back. This is noted. Not discussed.

## The factions

**The Kalevala Accord** — the governing body of the Expanse. Maintains the routes,
regulates the stations, argues about hehku policy. Fading authority. Maintains the forms
of governance without always having the substance.

Named itself after an idea of Ara without knowing she was real.

**Pohjan Neito** — the Northern Maiden. Controls rationing at critical stations when
disruption hits. Named for something older than the Accord.

**The Thousand Eyes** — present everywhere, acknowledged by no one. Nobody knows what
they want. They're always watching. Do not explain this.

## The hehku disruption — how the economy works

When Hiljaisuus is disrupted:

Hehku flow across the Expanse is choked. Stations begin drawing down reserves faster
than resupply. Pohjan Neito begins rationing at critical stations. Prices spike at all
stations, proportional to their dependency on Hiljaisuus routes. When the disruption
clears, prices normalize — but not to baseline. The market has memory.

This is what Eerikki inadvertently causes. He attempts to fix it. He makes it worse
for everyone except himself. He feels genuine guilt and remorse. He keeps moving.

## The ur-myth — corrupted history

### What nobody knows

There is no famous scientist, no celebrated departure, no name attached to humanity's
transition from one world to the diaspora. All of that happened so long ago that
the origins are completely forgotten. What is remembered has already become the stuff
that gets laughed off as religion and myth. Even where people seriously believe in
religion — which happens on worlds — they never have any conscious thought about where
they actually came from.

The origin is gone. What remains is the story of the origin, worn into myth, worn
further into embarrassing folk belief that educated people find quaint and serious
believers hold without examining too closely.

### Earth

The least auspicious planet in the universe. Between the author and the reader:
it's where all these people came from. This is never stated in the novel. It is
obvious to the reader who is paying attention and irrelevant to everyone else.

### The Finns

The people who eventually left that world. Who discovered the brane. Who first
encountered hehku. This is also never stated. It is in the shape of the language,
the names, the cosmology, the register. The reader who knows will know. The reader
who doesn't will feel it without being able to name it.

### The plateau

Before brane travel, human civilizations in regular space were at a plateau for a
very long time. Interplanetary, maybe interstellar within reach. Technological as
they could be within regular physics. The plateau lasted long enough that it became
the assumed condition of existence.

Then brane travel was discovered and everything changed.

Hehku had the potential to end all strife and hunger and shortage. Worlds never got
past their ceiling with it. The amount of hehku worlds use is minuscule. The stationborn
marvel at this. The void may be running short. The worlds could have an endless supply
if only they used it correctly. They stop short every time. Every world. Every time.
The ceiling is in their myths as curse and taboo and divine prohibition — which are
the words for *we don't know why this happens but it always happens* in a language
that has forgotten it once had better words.

### The ur-myth

Every world has a version of the same myth. Details vary. The shape is identical.

A people from a cold place at the edge of everything. A discovery that shouldn't have
been possible. A departure. A gift of abundance that came with a catch nobody fully
understood. Beings who moved between worlds and didn't stay. A silence at the center
of everything that has a name.

*Hiljaisuus.*

The silence has been in the myths the whole time. Not as a place. As the thing that
was there before the departure. The thing they left behind, or carried with them,
or that followed without being invited.

### What the myth actually is

Not myth. Corrupted history.

The story of how humans left one world, discovered the brane, found hehku, built the
diaspora — that story is so old it has completed the full cycle:

*History → legend → myth → religion → embarrassing folk belief*

The names have changed. The technology is described in the vocabulary of magic because
the vocabulary of the technology has been lost so long that nobody remembers it was
technology. The singers who carried the story forward didn't know they were carrying
history. They thought they were singing about gods.

They were singing about the stationborn.
They were singing about the void.
They were singing about hehku, which they called by other names, all of which meant
the same thing: the gift that came from beyond, that glowed, that could end all want,
that nobody fully understood.

### The stationborn relationship to the ur-myth

They learn comparative mythology in school. Find it mildly interesting the way you
find mildly interesting that multiple unconnected cultures independently invented the
wheel. Convergent cultural evolution. Explicable. Moving on.

Reko and Turo had this class. Neither of them remembers much of it.

### What Selli knows

It isn't convergent cultural evolution.

The ur-myth is the same on every world because it is the same event on every world.
The departure. The discovery. The diaspora. The hehku. All of it happened once and
the echo of it is in every worldborn culture because every worldborn culture descended
from the people it happened to.

Selli knows this.

The vocabulary required to say it out loud is the vocabulary of worldborn superstition.

Selli won't say it.

The paddle clicks.

### Why this is everything

The Kalevala — the runolaulu, the Finnish oral tradition Lönnrot collected in Karelia
in the 1830s — is in the ur-myth. Not as an influence. As the origin. The songs
Väinämöinen sang. The kantele made from a giant pike's jawbone. The departure across
water toward something unknown. The silence at the edge of the world that has always
been there.

The novel's backdrop is not inspired by Finnish mythology.
The novel's backdrop *is* Finnish mythology, at sufficient distance in time that it
has become the ur-myth every world carries without knowing where it came from.

This is the mythological foundation becoming a real universe.

## The four epistemic scales

**Worldborn** — a life is seventy, eighty years. A generation is twenty. A civilization
is a thousand years of noisy information. Present tense to the worldborn. Ancient history
to everyone else before the last person who lived it has finished dying.

**Stationborn** — worldborn generations are seasons. Civilizations are mildly interesting
weather patterns. The individual worldborn is invisible at this scale. The swarm has
patterns. The patterns are noted. The individual resolves into nothing.

**Voidbound** — Eerikki has watched stationborn cultures drift. The Accord that exists
now is not the Accord he first encountered. He remembers when current assumptions weren't
obvious. This makes him slightly uncanny — he is measuring the present against something
stationborn can't see.

**Ara** — the brane itself has rhythms from her frame. The routes through Hiljaisuus are
not permanent. She watched them settle into their current configuration. She knows what
came before. She knows what will come after. She knows the boundary Eerikki is looking
for is not where he thinks it is. She knows what it actually is. He hasn't asked the
right question.

## The stationborn as gods

Worldborn myths describe beings who live forever, move between worlds, are indifferent
to human suffering not from cruelty but from scale incompatibility. Who intervene
seemingly at random. Who have their own concerns that leave permanent marks on worldborn
history without apparent intention.

They made those myths out of need. Out of terror of the indifferent universe.

And then there are the stationborn.

Who are not gods. Who know they are not gods. Who have shift work and irritating
colleagues and the specific anxiety of people who live close to hard vacuum. Who die,
eventually, just more slowly.

But from the worldborn frame: these beings live forever, move between worlds without
aging, are indifferent to worldborn concerns, appear in worldborn history and vanish,
leave permanent marks without intention.

The myth fits. Perfectly.

The stationborn find this absolutely mortifying. Reko and Turo would find it hilarious
for thirty seconds and then it would start to bother them and they'd change the subject.
Selli has a joke about it. Has never told it. The vocabulary required costs too much.

Eerikki doesn't find it mortifying. He finds it clarifying. He was worldborn once.
He has been on both sides of this mirror.

## Ananta Shesha

The brane as the skin of something vast that is also alive. The hehku as what moves
through it. Held lightly — not as theology, as a framework that fits.

Aava has the closest relationship to this. She perceives pattern underneath things,
not spirits in things. The void is the most legible place she has ever been.
