# Kaiku

*Echo of the void.*

A free FM synthesizer for Linux, macOS, and Windows.
VST3. REAPER-tested. KVR Music Cafe.

---

Kaiku synthesizes two things that don't exist inside it:
a hurdy gurdy wheel, and a human mouth.

It does this through FM operators — mathematics approximating
the physics of friction and resonance. The approximation
is intentional. The instrument is not trying to be
those things. It is trying to remember their shape.

The Tyhjyydenkaiku patch — the primary voice — runs
three parallel FM stacks. The first shapes the vowel
body: the formant character of a mouth mid-syllable.
The second shapes the nasal upper partial. The third
runs at 1.008× the fundamental, eight cents sharp,
creating a slow beat against the first carrier.
That beating is the wheel. It was always imperfect.
That imperfection is why it sounds alive.

On accented notes, a fourth voice fires and decays
in eighty milliseconds. No sustain. The bark of the
trompette bridge. The hurdy's rhythmic accent.
It doesn't announce itself. It arrives.

## The acoustic spaces

Two synthetic impulse responses. Both generated by `hiljaisuus_ir.py`.
Load into any convolution reverb.

**`hiljaisuus_station.wav`** — 40×12×7 meter metal station corridor.
RT60 2.2 seconds. Reflection coefficient 0.924. The far wall arrives
at 221ms; that late return is what makes the space read as a corridor
rather than a room. Primary room for the KAAMOS record — both sides.
Kaiku was designed to live here.

**`voidborn_forest.wav`** — Karelian boreal forest interior.
RT60 0.62 seconds. Reflection coefficient 0.636. Pine, spruce, birch.
Moss floor. Canopy at 11 meters. Energy absorption 59.6% — organic
surfaces eat sound; metal reflects it. Primary room for the Éamonn
an Chnoic voidborn recording. Available as a secondary layer on
Side B birch flute voices where the station reads too hard.

The station is infrastructure. The forest is alive.
Both rooms are load-bearing.

## The record

One record. Two tracks. Side A / Side B.

The format reference is 12" vinyl, 1982. This is a production
constraint and an aesthetic — not a release target. The record
will not be pressed. The vinyl standard is what the mix is
designed against.

1982 is the last year analog production reigned without contest.
The CD launched that year; streaming didn't exist; nobody
competed for playlist loudness. The loud parts were loud against
genuinely quiet ones. Dynamic range was used, not surrendered.
What was recorded was committed. This is the production ethic.
Not nostalgia.

**Side A** (~4:35) — F# Phrygian. Opens on a held drone before
rhythm condenses out of the silence. Tyhjyydenkaiku is the primary
melodic voice. Antiphonal synthetic chorus. A sung verse in Finnish,
Kalevala meter. Ends on a threshold instrument cut to silence,
then birch flute descent into the void.

**Side B** (*Toinen raita* — working title: The Second Track) —
the birch flute in its paired form. Two voices moving in parallel
thirds throughout, not trading phrases but moving together.
Triple meter. Texture transparent against Side A's density.
The dynamic ceiling is not breached. Side B sits nearer the label;
the inner-groove degradation is a production fact, not something
to engineer around.

## Studio method

The 1982 production ethic is not hiss and phono click dropped
into a digital mix. That is the costume. The method is:

**Saturation on the buss, not the tracks.** If tape emulation goes
anywhere, it goes on the sum. The intermodulation between elements
happens when they hit the medium together. Track-level tape
emulation is the wrong layer.

**Dynamic range left in.** No compression to streaming standard.
The noise floor is the bottom; the music breathes above it.
Design music that uses the range.

**One room, committed.** The acoustic space is consistent across
both sides. Reverb is space, not texture. The space is not
automated — it has a signature and stops there.

**Mono below 150Hz.** Wide stereo low end causes lacquer groove
failure. More practically: the mono constraint forces clarity.
The bass is a mono decision. It doesn't hide in width.

**Frequency ceiling warm around 14kHz.** Presence lives in 2–8kHz.
Designing for brightness above 14kHz is designing for a format
that didn't exist in 1982. The top end is warm because the chain
conspired to make it warm — cutting head, cartridge, pressing,
tone arm. That is the target, not a limitation to compensate for.

**Performance committed.** Mix rides as real-time gestures.
The human hand is in the mix as a consequence of the workflow.
Do not automate back to precision what was alive in the ride.

**The 24-track discipline.** Every slot costs something. Sparseness
is not an aesthetic choice in this context — it is the natural
consequence of scarcity. The discipline of not adding more
because you can is the 1982 ethic applied to a DAW session.

## Toolchain

**DAW:** REAPER on Linux (primary). macOS/ARM64 and REAPER JS port
are stretch targets.

**Instruments:** Kaiku (this plugin) for the primary FM voice.
Koivuhuilu and Kuolinvihellys voices handled by separate
instruments — documentation pending.

**Effects:** Voxengo tape emulation on the buss. Arturia VST FX
for signal chain processing. Open to the full KVR plugin ecosystem
for any layer the music asks for.

**Reverb:** Convolution via `hiljaisuus_ir.py` — two IRs, two rooms.
Station corridor (`hiljaisuus_station.wav`) for the KAAMOS record.
Voidborn forest (`voidborn_forest.wav`) for the Éamonn an Chnoic
voidborn recording and Side B secondary space.

## Formation roots

Louis and Bebe Barron (*Forbidden Planet*, 1956): circuits as
organisms. The instrument has a lifespan. The imperfection is
the instrument. Tyhjyydenkaiku's 8-cent detuning is in this lineage.

Raymond Scott: the Electronium (1959–1987, unfinished). A device
that generates music autonomously — not a synthesizer you play.
Scott spent three decades on it and considered it unfinished at
his death. The machine that composes is not a solved problem.
It never has been.

The composers of the CoCo games had speech synthesis and multipart
harmony available to them and underexploited both. The hardware
was there. The vision wasn't. A composer with current knowledge
applied to 1982 hardware can produce something nobody produced
in 1982. That is the method here. The limitation as the vocabulary,
not the apology.

## Build

See `BUILD.md`.

Primary target: REAPER on Linux. Two reasons for that priority:
building a VST3 for Linux means understanding the stack at a depth
Windows abstracts away; and professional audio on Linux is underserved
and worth serving. Kaiku ships for Linux first. It also builds for
Windows and macOS — the instrument is for whoever wants to play it.

Stretch: REAPER on macOS/ARM64, REAPER JS port.

## Technical

- 6-operator FM, fixed algorithm (3 parallel stacks)
- 16-voice polyphony
- Velocity-triggered trompette layer
- APVTS parameter state (DAW automation ready)
- KaamOS GUI aesthetic: amber on void black, hex motifs,
  no gradients, no rounded corners, Hiljaisuus pulse

---

## Context

Kaiku is part of the Kaamos project — a novel, a game,
and a desktop environment that share a visual and
sonic vocabulary. The instrument was built to serve
two tracks on one record and released here because
instruments should be played.

The worldborn didn't build it. It arrived with the
first settlers and nobody replaced it because it worked.
