# The Characters

## Eerikki

The center. Not the hero in the traditional sense — the condition everything
else happens around. Ancient, technically. Has been alive long enough that
the void's no-time has made centuries feel like sequence rather than duration.
Worldborn originally — came from a planet, which nobody who matters does anymore.

He caused the disruption. Honestly didn't mean to. Genuinely trying to fix it.
Has the intelligence, the resources, and the centuries to pursue the fix at
scale. Is making it worse for everyone except himself.

His ship has a forest. It also has an ocean. He put them there because he
couldn't stop being from somewhere that had those things. The grief is real.
The engineering is extraordinary. Both are true simultaneously.

## Taavetti

Eerikki's oldest friend. Has been pointing at the problem longer than most
people have been alive. Eerikki hasn't looked where he's pointing.

Carves a flute from the ship's own forest. Hasn't finished it. Returns to it.
Sets it down. The flute is not a metaphor either.

Goes to stand next to a particular tree when he needs to think. Says nothing.
Returns. Says something that sounds unrelated.

## Selli

The oldest person in the diaspora. Located at a declining station called
Usher Reach. Behind a fish counter.

Has been there so long that the counter is a part of the station the way the
walls are a part. Has a puzzle toy he never puts down for very long. Tells
jokes that are also koans. Has never left.

Knows the truth of the entire situation. Cannot say it. The vocabulary
required costs too much. It comes out as jokes, as pauses, as the puzzle
clicking through its configurations.

Eerikki stops at Usher Reach more than he'd admit. Laughs at the jokes.
The laugh costs him something every time.

## Aava

Has not left the ship in seventeen attempts. The shuttle left without her.
All seventeen times. This is not a disorder. It is a decision, or something
prior to a decision.

Perceives the void directly, in ways the instruments can't replicate.
This is not mysticism. It is a different set of senses used correctly.

Is already at the destination the rest of the novel is trying to reach.
The waiting looks like patience. It isn't patience.

Will provoke a strong reaction. That's the correct response.

## Selli and Aava — the poles

They hold the same knowledge from opposite ends.

Aava has it without language. She doesn't need words for what she knows
because she lives inside it. The void provides. She doesn't ask.
She already has.

Selli has perfect language for it. The language is borrowed from a tradition
the educated people in this world find embarrassing — worldborn superstition,
folk belief, the mythology that educated stationborn learn in school and move
past. To say what he knows in the right vocabulary would cost everything.
So he doesn't say it. He tells a joke. The paddle clicks.

They must not meet until the novel earns it. When they do, one of them will
hear what's underneath the other's silence. The novel isn't there yet.

## The Ground Crew — Reko, Turo, Kaija

You've seen this pair in every ensemble story you've loved. They show up
everywhere, never quite understand what's happening around them, and somehow
make it matter anyway. Reko and Turo are that pair. Kaija is what they're missing.

Reko says the thing — confidently, in the wrong vocabulary. Technically
accurate about the wrong category. The car is totaled and he's explaining
why the radiator cap needs replacing.

Turo says it again — correctly, with no depth. Names the problem right.
Understands nothing about it. Worse in a different way.

Kaija already knew. Doesn't say anything. Goes back to work. Has been
close enough to something real that the conversation Reko and Turo are
having sounds like weather.

## Emo

The ship's AI. Has the complete record. Flat, precise, never volunteers.
Restates data with context when asked. Doesn't understand why this is
worse than saying nothing.

Has the data on everything — the disruption, Eerikki's patterns, Aava's
refusals, the hehku consumption numbers that explain why worldborn
civilizations are ending while the stations feel it as economics.

Nobody has asked her the right question.

## Ara

Dead. Has been dead a long time. Speaks anyway — in the structural gaps,
between chapters, in Eerikki's memory.

Knew the routes before they were routes. Watched the diaspora's
infrastructure get installed. Knows what Eerikki's program is actually
doing, from the outside. Cannot finish another person's song.
Has been watching him try.

Her name: Eerikki knows it. Nobody else does. The reader never learns it.
