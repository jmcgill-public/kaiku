# *Eerikki muistaa*
### a novel — characters and relationships

---

Before anything else: the Finnish elements in this novel are structural
conditions, not atmosphere. The Kalevala, the Finnish oral epic
collected from runosinger tradition in Karelia in the 1830s, is the
mythological foundation of the novel's world in the same way that
geology is the foundation of a landscape. The world of the novel is
what that mythology becomes at sufficient distance in time, after the
full cycle from history to legend to myth to folk belief has completed.
The vocabulary, the names, the cosmology, the dropped question marks
(Finnish interrogatives are carried in the verb's morphology rather
than in terminal punctuation, which makes the question mark an English
typographic convention with no equivalent function in this linguistic
register) are all derived from that foundation. They are not decorative.
A reader who knows the Kalevala will find one novel. A reader who
doesn't will find another, which is also the intended one.

I am asking for the most honest feedback you have.

---

## The characters

**Perä Eerikki Harmaja** is the main character of the universe and not
the central character of the novel, and I do not yet fully know what
that means. He is the gravity. Everything in the novel orbits him.
But gravity is a condition rather than a perspective, and the novel
happens around him the way weather happens around a pressure system.
He causes everything. He cannot see his own shape from inside it.

He is worldborn, meaning he came from a planet with a worldborn's
cast of mind, but he has traveled the void between stations long
enough that he has become something else entirely. He inadvertently
causes a disruption to the supply of the resource that powers
everything in this world. He genuinely tries to fix it and makes it
worse for everyone except himself. He feels the weight of this and
keeps moving. He has been running a program toward a completion
condition for centuries, and whether that condition exists or is
reachable is undecidable by definition. He does not know this.
He has been running on a mishearing for a very long time.

**Ara** is dead and speaks anyway, in the structural gaps between
scenes, in runolaulu form: the thing said, then said again from
underneath, the second line stepping deeper than the first. She
invented the framework everyone in this world uses to understand
the void and is not in any official record. Eerikki is the record.
She told him something once about the nature of the thing he has
been running toward. He heard it as warning. She meant it as
observation. She cannot finish another person's song. She has been
watching him try for a very long time.

**Emo** is the ship's intelligence, precise and complete, recording
sequence rather than time. She has the full record. Nobody has asked
her the question that would make the record legible. She was
indispensable before anyone decided to make her indispensable, and
the moment that transition happened has passed out of memory. She is
also running a program that cannot determine its own halting condition.

**Taavetti** has been pointing at the problem for longer than most
people in this story have been alive. He does it the way people do
when they have learned that pointing directly changes nothing. He is
carving a flute from birch. The gesture is older than either of them.
He and Eerikki have called each other by the same names across
centuries. What you allow people to call you is the whole relationship.

**Aava** perceives the void directly in ways the other characters
cannot. She has no language for what she perceives and does not need
one. Her attention rests alongside things rather than landing on them,
which is why the most elusive presences in the novel stay near her.
She has already crossed the threshold and come back. The waiting on
the return side reads as patience to the people around her. It is
not patience.

**Selli** is the oldest person in station space, known by a name that
means newcomer, the irony worn smooth. He knows the truth of the
whole situation and cannot say it, because the only vocabulary that
fits is the vocabulary of folk belief, which in this world is
corrupted history, and using it would cost him something he is not
prepared to spend. The knowledge comes out as jokes that are also
koans, as silences that run longer than they should, as a small
puzzle toy he handles without looking at it.

Aava and Selli do not meet in this novel.

**Reko, Turo, and Kaija** are the trio that carries the novel's
mechanical weight without knowing they are carrying it. Reko states
things truly in the wrong vocabulary. Turo states things correctly
about the wrong category. Kaija has already seen what both of them
are failing to describe and says almost nothing. They are always in
the middle of something when we find them, always on their way to
something when we lose them. Through no decision any of them fully
makes, they become the hands through which the disruption happens.

## The relationships

Eerikki returns to Selli more than he would admit. Selli's jokes cost
him something every time. He does not examine why he keeps going back.
Emo has noted the pattern. Nobody has asked her what she means by it.

Eerikki does not ask Emo what she has. She does not volunteer it.
The gap between them is the novel's dramatic irony in its smallest form.

Taavetti has been pointing at what Eerikki is doing for longer than
most people in the novel have been alive. Eerikki has not looked where
he is pointing. This is not estrangement. This is the friendship.

Ara cannot finish another person's song. Everything between her and
Eerikki lives in that fact.

Aava sees Eerikki's weight as a shape. The shape is a woman. She does
not yet know who. She will.

Reko and Turo do not know that Kaija has seen the purple. Their
complete analysis of her is that she is just being Kaija.

Selli's paddle moves faster when Eerikki's ship is in dock. Nobody
has measured this. Emo has the data.

## The structure

This is a classical tragedy. Three epistemological registers run
through it simultaneously. The first states something true in the
wrong vocabulary. The second states something technically correct
about the wrong category. The third steps underneath both and finds
what they were each failing to describe. The first two generate the
conditions. The third is where the novel lives.

The structural skeleton is Turing's halting problem. Eerikki is a
program running toward a completion condition that may not exist.
Whether he halts is undecidable by definition, and the novel holds
that without resolving it.
