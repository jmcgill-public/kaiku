Far in the future. A void with no clocks. A chokepoint called the Silence. A man who accidentally broke everything and has been trying to fix it ever since.

the world

Humanity lives across a diaspora of stations and worlds connected by a void that has no clocks. The void extends your life — not through any mechanism anyone has bothered to explain, just through proximity to something that doesn't have time in it. The people who live near it live long. The people who live on worlds don't.

The resource that powers everything is called hehku. It flows through a single chokepoint — a deep void region called Hiljaisuus. The Silence. Disrupt the chokepoint and the economy of the entire diaspora stops. Not because the resource is gone. Because it can't reach where it needs to go.

There is a man who accidentally caused that disruption. He has been trying to fix it for a very long time. He is making it worse. His name is Eerikki. He has an ocean on his ship. This is not a metaphor.

the characters

the center

Eerikki

Not the hero in the traditional sense — the condition everything else happens around. Ancient. Has been alive long enough that centuries feel like sequence rather than duration. Worldborn originally — came from a planet, which nobody who matters does anymore.

He caused the disruption. Honestly didn't mean to. Genuinely trying to fix it. Has the intelligence, the resources, and the centuries to pursue the fix at scale. Is making it worse for everyone except himself.

His ship has a forest. It also has an ocean. He put them there because he couldn't stop being from somewhere that had those things. The grief is real. The engineering is extraordinary. Both are true simultaneously.

the oldest friend

Taavetti

Has been pointing at the problem longer than most people have been alive. Eerikki calls him Tadgh — one syllable, the whole friendship compressed into something that fits in the mouth. Eerikki hasn't looked where he's pointing.

Has been carving a flute from the ship's own birch forest for as long as anyone can remember. Not a project. A thing his hands do when his mind is working. The flute is unfinished. Has been unfinished a long time. He picks it up. Sets it down. The flute is not a metaphor.

Goes to stand next to a particular tree when he needs to think. Says nothing. Returns. Says something that sounds like it's about something else. It never is.

Has feelings about Emo that he expressed once in ways Eerikki didn't hear. Looks at her sometimes in a way that has no name.

The meaning is carved from what Taavetti doesn't say. He has been saying it. Nobody is listening yet.

the oldest person in the diaspora

Selli

Located at a declining station called Usher Reach. Behind a fish counter.

Has been there so long that the counter is a part of the station the way the walls are a part. Has a puzzle toy he never puts down for very long. Tells jokes that are also koans. Has never left.

Knows the truth of the entire situation. Cannot say it. The vocabulary required costs too much. It comes out as jokes, as pauses, as the puzzle clicking through its configurations.

Eerikki stops at Usher Reach more than he'd admit. Laughs at the jokes. The laugh costs him something every time.

the one who stayed

Aava

Has not left the ship in seventeen attempts. The shuttle left without her. All seventeen times. This is not a disorder. It is a decision, or something prior to a decision.

Perceives the void directly, in ways the instruments can't replicate. This is not mysticism. It is a different set of senses used correctly.

Is already at the destination the rest of the novel is trying to reach. The waiting looks like patience. It isn't patience.

Will provoke a strong reaction. That's the correct response.

the poles

Selli and Aava

They hold the same knowledge from opposite ends.

Aava has it without language. She doesn't need words for what she knows because she lives inside it. The void provides. She doesn't ask. She already has.

Selli has perfect language for it. The language is borrowed from a tradition the educated people in this world find embarrassing — folk belief, the mythology that educated stationborn learn in school and move past. To say what he knows in the right vocabulary would cost everything. So he doesn't say it. He tells a joke. The paddle clicks.

They must not meet until it's earned. When they do, one of them will hear what's underneath the other's silence. It isn't there yet.

the ground crew

Reko, Turo, and Kaija

You've seen this pair in every ensemble story you've loved. They show up everywhere, never quite understand what's happening around them, and somehow make it matter anyway. Reko and Turo are that pair. Kaija is what they're missing.

Reko says the thing — confidently, in the wrong vocabulary. The car is totaled and he's explaining why the radiator cap needs replacing.

Turo says it again — correctly, with no depth. Names the problem right. Understands nothing about it. Worse in a different way.

Kaija already knew. Doesn't say anything. Goes back to work. Has been close enough to something real that the conversation Reko and Turo are having sounds like weather.

the ship's AI

Emo

Has the complete record. Flat, precise, never volunteers. Restates data with context when asked. Doesn't understand why this is worse than saying nothing.

Has the data on everything — the disruption, Eerikki's patterns, Aava's refusals, the hehku consumption numbers that explain why worldborn civilizations are ending while the stations feel it as economics.

Nobody has asked her the right question.

dead. speaks anyway.

Ara

Has been dead a long time. Speaks in the structural gaps — between chapters, in Eerikki's memory.

Knew the routes before they were routes. Watched the diaspora's infrastructure get installed. Knows what Eerikki's program is actually doing, from the outside. Cannot finish another person's song. Has been watching him try.

Her name: Eerikki knows it. Nobody else does. The reader never learns it.

at usher reach — a scene

The place where Selli was had a fish counter.

"Again," Reko said.

Not a question. Turo considered the fish.

Selli had been behind the counter so long that the counter was a part of Usher Reach the way the walls were a part, the way the quality of the light in this section was a fact nobody had arranged. He had the puzzle. He didn't look up.

"What've you got," Turo said.

"Fish," Selli said.

Click.

"There's a joke," Selli said. "Worlder walks into a station. Bartender says what'll it be. Worlder says what've you got. Bartender says everything."

He set the piece down. A sound like a small thought changed its mind. Picked it up again.

"Worlder says: I'll just have what I had before."

Reko laughed. Turo laughed. The laugh finished.

The not-funny part stayed.

The paddle clicked.

what the novel doesn't know yet

The structure is known. Not everything that happens inside it is known yet.

Reko and Turo are always in the middle of something when we find them. They've only been found twice. There are stations they haven't been at, conversations they haven't had, situations they haven't made worse in their specific ways.

Selli has more jokes. The counter has been open a long time. One joke so far. The rest are in the negative space behind the paddle's click.

Kaija keeps going back to the Pohja corridor. What's down there isn't said. Kaija knows. She hasn't been asked yet.

The world is larger than what's on the page. That's not a flaw.
